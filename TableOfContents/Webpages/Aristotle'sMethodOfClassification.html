Classification of animals as seen by Aristotle

Aristotle, often called the "Father of Zoology," was one of the first to attempt a systematic classification of animals. He divided animals into two main groups based on the presence or absence of blood: “blooded” animals (which included vertebrates) and “bloodless” animals (invertebrates). This basic division helped him distinguish major types of animals and laid the foundation for future classification systems.

He also grouped animals according to their habitat, classifying them as land-dwellers, water-dwellers, or air-dwellers. Within these groups, he observed physical features, modes of reproduction, and movement patterns to further categorize them. Although his system was simple and sometimes inaccurate by modern standards, it was a major step toward understanding the diversity of life in a logical way.
